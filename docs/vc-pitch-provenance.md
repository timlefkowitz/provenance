# Provenance — Pitch Brief

*For collectors, institutions, and partners who care about authenticity, history, and operational excellence.*

---

## One-line thesis

**Provenance turns artwork history into structured, verifiable data**—so artists are recognized fairly, institutions run modern collections workflows, and the market can rely on a **single, inspectable chain of evidence** instead of fragmented PDFs and oral tradition.

---

## The problem (why this matters at $100M painting scale)

- **Value is not only “who painted it.”** At the highest end, price is a function of **title, exhibition history, literature, condition narrative, ownership chain, and dispute history**—i.e., **provenance** and **documentation quality**.
- Today, that evidence lives across **emails, PDFs, spreadsheets, gallery systems, and private notes**—hard to audit, hard to share under NDA, and hard to insure or lend against with confidence.
- **Artists** often lose economic upside because their professional history is **under-documented** relative to how the secondary market prices comparable works.
- **Museums and foundations** need **collection management** that is not just cataloging, but **workflow, compliance, loans, conservation notes, and verification** in a world of increasing scrutiny.

---

## What Provenance does

Provenance is building a **next-generation platform** where artwork records, provenance events, certificates, and institutional workflows can be captured **consistently**, **permissioned**, and where relevant, **verified**—with APIs that let partners plug into the same truth layer.

---

## Chapter 1 — Helping artists: value through provenance, not just promotion

### The insight

For many artists, the gap between “strong career” and “strong market” is **not talent**—it is **documented career**: exhibitions, collections, publications, institutional acquisitions, conservation history, and clear ownership transfers.

### How Provenance helps

- **Structured provenance as an asset**: Turn exhibition history, publications, gallery relationships, and certificate events into a **durable record** that supports appraisal, lending, and resale narratives.
- **Fairer comparables**: When an artist’s trajectory is captured with dates, venues, and references, **pricing discussions** move from anecdote to **evidence-backed** storytelling (without replacing appraisers—**supporting** them).
- **Professional operations**: Grants, open calls, portfolio and CV workflows—so artists spend less time retyping the same biography into fifty forms.

### What we are *not* claiming

We are not promising “the algorithm sets the price.” We are building the **infrastructure** that makes **valuation and market analysis** more grounded—especially as works circulate.

## Chapter 2 — Helping institutions: a next-gen application for collections and museum tasks

### What “next gen” means here

Not “another database,” but a system aligned with how museums actually work:

- **Collection truth + task truth**: Objects, locations, loans, approvals, conservation notes, exhibition histories—**linked** and **permissioned**.
- **Operational workflows**: internal review, approvals, responsibilities—reducing institutional risk and email overhead.
- **Interoperability**: exports and APIs so the collection can connect to conservation tools, digital asset management, and partner lenders—**without** locking the institution into a dead-end silo.

### Institutional outcomes

- **Faster, safer loans and exhibitions** when provenance packets are consistent and complete.
- **Better governance** for sensitive information (who can see what, when, under what agreement).
- **Foundation for digital programs**—public-facing storytelling grounded in authoritative internal records.

## Chapter 3 — The API: asset verification and trust at scale

### Asset verification API (anchor use case)

A programmatic layer to support partners who need to answer:

- “Is this record **consistent** with what the issuer / owner / institution asserts?”
- “What **evidence bundle** exists (events, documents metadata, signatures, timestamps) subject to permissions?”

**Important framing for sophisticated buyers and counsel:** verification is strongest when it separates:

1. **Cryptographic / systems integrity** (tamper-evidence, timestamps, access logs)
2. **Human / institutional attestation** (who attested, under what role)
3. **Third-party corroboration** (literature, exhibition records) where available

Provenance can become the **rails** that connect these layers—especially for partners who must operationalize trust: insurers, lenders, logistics, and marketplaces.

---

## Chapter 4 — Profitable API use cases (beyond “check a box”)

### Tiered API products (examples)

- **Marketplace & auction integrations**: listing eligibility, provenance completeness score, required fields for premium placement.
- **Insurance & risk workflows**: standardized event history export, change logs, and permissioned disclosure to underwriters.
- **Logistics & chain-of-custody**: scan events, location attestations, handoffs—especially for high-value transit.
- **Lending & collateral**: evidence packages for lenders evaluating art as collateral (process, not a price oracle).
- **Institutional collaboration**: federated sharing between museums, lenders, and consignors under strict ACLs.
- **Data products (ethical, opt-in)**: aggregated benchmarks on documentation completeness by segment (not “secret prices,” but **documentation maturity** indices), valuable to trade organizations and insurers.

### Why APIs can be *more* profitable than seats alone

- **High-trust integrations** have low churn when embedded in operational workflows.
- **Usage-based pricing** aligns with partner scale (events verified, API calls, workspaces).
- **Enterprise contracts** with clear SLAs fit museums, insurers, and marketplaces.

- Digital art commerce grew, but **physical art’s paperwork didn’t modernize equally**.
- Institutions face rising expectations for **accountability**, **accession rigor**, and **digital publishing**.
- Collectors at the top end increasingly want **defensible diligence**—not another glossy JPEG.
